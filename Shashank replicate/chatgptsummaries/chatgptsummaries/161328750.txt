The petitioner in this case had requested the summoning of an interrogation note of the complainant, which was rejected by the trial court. The petitioner then filed a revision petition, which was also rejected.

The court, upon hearing the case, found that the trial court had not committed any manifest error in rejecting the petitioner's request for the summoning of the interrogation note. The court noted that the trial court had considered the relevant provisions of law and had applied them correctly in rejecting the petitioner's request.

The court further observed that the petitioner had not been able to demonstrate any prejudice that would result from the trial court's decision to not summon the interrogation note. The court noted that the petitioner had not been able to show how the interrogation note was relevant to the case or how it would have helped the petitioner's defense.

In light of these observations, the court dismissed the petitioner's revision petition and advised the petitioner to produce their defense before the trial court. The court emphasized that the trial court was the appropriate forum for the petitioner to present their defense and that the trial court would consider all relevant evidence in making its decision.
