The court recently granted bail to Raju Nath, who had been arrested in connection with an offense under Section 9/51 of the Wild Life Protection Act. The petitioner had been in custody for over a month, and no other case for the same offense was registered against him. The court allowed the bail application under Section 439 Cr.P.C. and ordered the accused-petitioner to furnish a personal bond of Rs.50,000/- with two surety bonds of Rs.25,000/- each to the satisfaction of the learned trial court.

The Wild Life Protection Act is a crucial piece of legislation that aims to protect and conserve wildlife in India. It is a comprehensive law that covers a wide range of issues related to wildlife, including the protection of endangered species, the regulation of hunting and poaching, and the prevention of illegal trade in wildlife products. Section 9/51 of the Act deals with offenses related to hunting, poaching, or killing of wild animals, and provides for severe penalties for those found guilty of such offenses.

In this case, Raju Nath was arrested for an offense under Section 9/51 of the Act. He had been in custody for over a month, and no other case for the same offense was registered against him. The court, therefore, considered his bail application and granted him bail under Section 439 Cr.P.C. This section of the Criminal Procedure Code allows for the grant of bail to an accused person who is in custody during the pendency of the trial.

This is a standard requirement for the grant of bail, and it ensures that the accused person will appear in court for the trial and will not abscond. The surety bonds are provided by two persons who undertake to ensure that the accused person will appear in court when required.
