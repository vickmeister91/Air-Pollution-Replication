The Punjab and Haryana High Court recently issued a court order in response to a writ petition filed by M/s Gold Mine Stone Crusher. The petition challenged orders issued by the State of Punjab under the Air Prevention & Control of Pollution Act and the Water Prevention & Control of Pollution Act. The court directed the Appellate Authority to consider and decide the appeals filed by the petitioners against the orders expeditiously, preferably within two months. Until then, no coercive step should be taken against the petitioners.

The writ petition filed by M/s Gold Mine Stone Crusher challenged the orders issued by the State of Punjab under the Air Prevention & Control of Pollution Act and the Water Prevention & Control of Pollution Act. The petitioners argued that the orders were arbitrary, illegal, and without jurisdiction. They also contended that the orders were passed without giving them an opportunity to be heard.

The court noted that the petitioners had filed appeals before the Appellate Authority against the orders. However, the appeals had not been decided for a long time. The court observed that the delay in deciding the appeals was causing prejudice to the petitioners. Therefore, the court directed the Appellate Authority to consider and decide the appeals expeditiously, preferably within two months.

The court also directed that until the appeals were decided, no coercive step should be taken against the petitioners. This means that the State of Punjab cannot take any action against the petitioners based on the orders that are being challenged in the appeals. The court's order provides relief to the petitioners and ensures that they are not subjected to any adverse consequences until their appeals are decided.

The court's order is significant because it emphasizes the importance of timely disposal of appeals. The court recognized that delays in deciding appeals can cause prejudice to the parties involved. Therefore, the court directed the Appellate Authority to decide the appeals expeditiously, preferably within two months. This direction is likely to have a positive impact on the functioning of the Appellate Authority and ensure that appeals are decided in a timely manner.
