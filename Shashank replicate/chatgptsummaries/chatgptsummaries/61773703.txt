The case in question involves a criminal revision petition filed by the petitioner, who challenged the dismissal of his application under Section 91 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 by the trial court. The petitioner had requested the medical examination reports of three injured persons to be produced on record by the prosecution. However, the trial court had dismissed the application, prompting the petitioner to challenge the order.

The High Court was tasked with reviewing the case and ultimately dismissed the petition, stating that the trial court had rightly dismissed the application and that no ground for interference was made out. This decision was based on a number of factors, including the nature of the application, the evidence presented by the prosecution, and the overall context of the case.

One of the key issues at play in this case was the petitioner's request for the medical examination reports of the three injured persons. The petitioner argued that these reports were crucial to his defense and that they should be produced on record by the prosecution. However, the trial court disagreed and dismissed the application.

The High Court ultimately agreed with the trial court's decision, stating that the application was not well-founded and that there was no reason to believe that the medical examination reports would have any significant impact on the case. The court noted that the prosecution had already presented ample evidence to support their case and that the medical examination reports were not necessary to establish guilt or innocence.

In addition to the issue of the medical examination reports, the High Court also considered a number of other factors in reaching its decision.
