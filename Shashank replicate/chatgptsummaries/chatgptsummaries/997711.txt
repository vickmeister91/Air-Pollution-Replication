The case at hand involves a challenge to an order passed by the Sessions Judge, Vidisha, in Criminal Revision No. 82/1986 dated 25-8-1986. The petitioner's jeep and three licensed rifles were seized by the Range Forest Officer Gyaraspur under Section 50(1) of the Wild Life (Protection) Act, 1972. The petitioner filed an application before the Judicial Magistrate, First Class, Vidisha, for the return of the seized articles on supurdgi. The Judicial Magistrate, First Class, Vidisha, directed the articles to be returned, on interim custody, to the petitioner on his furnishing solvent surety of Rs. 1 lac. The Forest Department challenged this order, and the Sessions Judge, Vidisha, allowed the criminal revision. The petitioner invoked the inherent powers of the Court, and the impugned order was quashed, and the order passed by the Judicial Magistrate, First Class, Vidisha, was restored.

The petitioner filed an application before the Judicial Magistrate, First Class, Vidisha, for the return of the seized articles on supurdgi. The Judicial Magistrate, First Class, Vidisha, directed the articles to be returned, on interim custody, to the petitioner on his furnishing solvent surety of Rs. 1 lac.

The Forest Department challenged this order, and the Sessions Judge, Vidisha, allowed the criminal revision. The petitioner then invoked the inherent powers of the Court, and the impugned order was quashed, and the order passed by the Judicial Magistrate, First Class, Vidisha, was restored.

The petitioner's argument was that the Sessions Judge had erred in allowing the criminal revision, as the order passed by the Judicial Magistrate, First Class, Vidisha, was in accordance with the law. The petitioner contended that the Forest Department had failed to establish that the seizure of the petitioner's jeep and three licensed rifles was justified under Section 50(1) of the Wild Life (Protection) Act, 1972.

The Court, in its analysis, noted that the provisions of Section 50(1) of the Wild Life (Protection) Act, 1972, allowed for the seizure of any vehicle, vessel or animal which is used for committing an offence under the Act. However, the Court also noted that the provisions of Section 50(1) of the Act must be read in conjunction with Section 50(2) of the Act, which provides that any vehicle, vessel or animal seized under Section 50(1) shall be produced before the nearest Magistrate, who shall pass such orders as he may deem fit.

The Court further noted that the provisions of Section 50(2) of the Act were mandatory in nature, and that the Forest Department had failed to comply with the same. The Court observed that the Forest Department had not produced the seized articles before the nearest Magistrate, and had instead retained custody of the same.

The Court, therefore, held that the order passed by the Judicial Magistrate, First Class, Vidisha, was in accordance with the law, and that the Sessions Judge had erred in allowing the criminal revision. The Court quashed the impugned order and restored the order passed by the Judicial Magistrate, First Class, Vidisha.
