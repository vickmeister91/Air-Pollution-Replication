In a recent court order, Mohsim Ali was convicted for violating the Wildlife (Protection) Act, 1972 by possessing wild birds without proper authorization. The accused was found guilty of contravening the provisions of section 9, 44 & 49 of the Act and was sentenced to three years of rigorous imprisonment and a fine of Rs 25,000. The court also ordered the confiscation and destruction of the case property. This order comes after the accused had previously been convicted for similar offenses and had already served 103 days in jail.

The Wildlife (Protection) Act, 1972 is a crucial piece of legislation that aims to protect and conserve India's wildlife. The Act provides for the protection of wild animals, birds, and plants, and also regulates hunting, poaching, and trade in wildlife. Section 9 of the Act prohibits the hunting of wild animals, while section 44 prohibits the possession of any wild animal, bird, or plant without proper authorization. Section 49 of the Act provides for the punishment for contravention of the provisions of the Act.

In this case, Mohsim Ali was found guilty of possessing wild birds without proper authorization, which is a clear violation of section 44 of the Act. The court found that the accused had knowingly and intentionally possessed the birds, and had not obtained the necessary permits or licenses. The court also noted that the accused had previously been convicted for similar offenses, which indicates a pattern of disregard for the law.

The court's decision to sentence the accused to three years of rigorous imprisonment and a fine of Rs 25,000 is a strong message to those who engage in illegal wildlife trade. The punishment is severe enough to act as a deterrent to others who may be considering similar offenses. The court's decision to order the confiscation and destruction of the case property is also significant, as it ensures that the birds will not be used for any illegal purposes in the future.
