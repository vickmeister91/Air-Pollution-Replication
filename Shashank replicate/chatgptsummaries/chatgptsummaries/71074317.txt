The court order summaries pertain to two separate cases involving complaints filed against companies for allegedly violating environmental laws. In the first case, M/s Wellworth Construction Pvt. Ltd. had filed a petition seeking the quashing of a complaint filed against them under Section 15 of the Environment Protection Act, 1986. The complaint alleged that the company had started construction on a project without obtaining prior environmental clearance from the Ministry of Environment and Forests. The court heard arguments from both sides and ultimately did not quash the complaint, allowing the trial to proceed.

In the second case, a complaint had been filed against a company for starting a construction project without obtaining prior environmental clearance. The company had obtained the clearance certificate before starting the project, and the complaint was filed after the certificate was issued. The court found the allegations in the complaint to be vague and quashed the complaint and all subsequent proceedings. The court cited the State of Haryana vs. Bhajan Lal case and stated that the power to quash a criminal proceeding should be exercised sparingly and only in rare cases.

The first case highlights the importance of obtaining prior environmental clearance before starting any construction project. The Environment Protection Act, 1986, mandates that any person or company undertaking any construction activity that is likely to have an adverse impact on the environment must obtain prior clearance from the Ministry of Environment and Forests. Failure to obtain such clearance can result in legal action, as was the case with M/s Wellworth Construction Pvt. Ltd.

The court's decision not to quash the complaint against the company sends a strong message that environmental laws must be taken seriously and that violators will be held accountable. The trial will now proceed, and the company will have to defend itself against the allegations made in the complaint.

In the second case, the court's decision to quash the complaint highlights the importance of clarity in legal proceedings. The court found the allegations in the complaint to be vague and lacking in specificity, which made it difficult for the company to defend itself. The court's decision to quash the complaint and all subsequent proceedings is in line with the principle of natural justice, which requires that legal proceedings be fair and just.

The court also cited the State of Haryana vs. Bhajan Lal case, which sets out the circumstances under which a criminal proceeding can be quashed. The court noted that the power to quash a criminal proceeding should be exercised sparingly and only in rare cases. This is because the quashing of a criminal proceeding can have serious consequences for both the accused and the complainant.
