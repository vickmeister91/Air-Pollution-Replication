The use of loudspeakers during religious festivals has been a contentious issue in India for many years. On one hand, festivals are an integral part of the country's cultural heritage, and the use of loudspeakers is seen as an important aspect of the celebrations. On the other hand, the use of loudspeakers has been linked to noise pollution, which can have serious health consequences for people living in the vicinity of the celebrations.

Recently, the State Government of Maharashtra filed a Notice of Motion to permit the use of loudspeakers during the Navratri festival in Mumbai until 1:00 a.m. The government argued that the festival is a special occasion that requires an exemption with certain limitations. However, the application was opposed by the petitioner's counsel, who argued that the use of loudspeakers during religious festivals is not required and violates the Environment (Protection) Act, 1986.

The court rejected the government's application, stating that it would only add to the existing noise pollution in the city and that the government should take appropriate steps to control noise pollution created by loudspeakers during festivals. The court also reiterated its previous directions to the State Government to issue necessary advertisements to educate the public about noise pollution and for the Police Commissioner to issue necessary instructions to concerned police stations to take appropriate action on complaints regarding noise pollution.

The court's decision is a significant victory for those who have been advocating for stricter regulations on the use of loudspeakers during religious festivals. It sends a clear message to the government and festival organizers that they cannot ignore the health concerns of the people living in the vicinity of the celebrations.
