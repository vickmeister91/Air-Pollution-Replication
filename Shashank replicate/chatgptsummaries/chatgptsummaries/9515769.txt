The court order in question pertains to a bail application filed by Kamal Singh under Section 439 of the Code of Criminal Procedure. Singh had been in custody since June 23, 2019, for allegedly possessing skin and body parts of a leopard, in violation of the Wild Life Protection Act. However, the court noted that no skin or body parts were seized from Singh's possession and that there was no probability of him absconding or tampering with evidence. Therefore, the court allowed Singh's bail application on the condition that he furnish a bail bond of Rs. 50,000 and comply with the provisions of Section 437(3) of the Cr.P.C.

The case against Kamal Singh was based on allegations that he had been found in possession of skin and body parts of a leopard, which is a protected species under the Wild Life Protection Act. However, the court noted that there was no evidence to support these allegations, as no skin or body parts were seized from Singh's possession. This lack of evidence was a key factor in the court's decision to grant Singh bail.

In addition to the lack of evidence, the court also noted that there was no probability of Singh absconding or tampering with evidence. This was an important consideration in the court's decision, as it is one of the key factors that courts consider when deciding whether to grant bail. The court's decision to grant bail to Singh was therefore based on a careful consideration of all the relevant factors in the case.

However, the court did impose certain conditions on Singh's bail. Specifically, he was required to furnish a bail bond of Rs. 50,000 and comply with the provisions of Section 437(3) of the Cr.P.C. This section of the code requires that a person released on bail must attend court on all the specified dates of the trial and not commit any offence while on bail. These conditions were imposed to ensure that Singh would comply with the legal process and not pose a risk to public safety.
