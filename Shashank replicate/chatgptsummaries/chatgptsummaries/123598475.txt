The Karnataka High Court recently passed a court order in a criminal petition filed by Sri Raghavendra J.D. and Sri B. Ramakrishna Sherigar against the State of Karnataka. The petitioners had sought to quash the proceedings initiated against them for allegedly felling trees in a reserved forest area. The court found that the offences alleged against the petitioners were non-cognizable offences and that the permission of the Magistrate had not been obtained before the registration of the FIR. Therefore, the court quashed the proceedings against the petitioners and acquitted them of the charges.

The court order lists the various sections of the Karnataka Forest Act, 1963 and the Wild Life Protection Act, 1972 under which certain offences are punishable. The Karnataka Forest Act, 1963 provides for the protection of forests and the regulation of felling of trees. Section 8 of the Act prohibits the felling of trees in reserved forests without the permission of the Forest Officer. Section 26 of the Act provides for punishment for contravention of the provisions of the Act, which includes imprisonment for a term which may extend to six months or a fine which may extend to Rs. 5000 or both.

The Wild Life Protection Act, 1972 provides for the protection of wild animals and birds and for matters connected therewith. Section 9 of the Act prohibits hunting of wild animals and birds. Section 51 of the Act provides for punishment for contravention of the provisions of the Act, which includes imprisonment for a term which may extend to three years or a fine which may extend to Rs. 25,000 or both.

The court order highlights the importance of obtaining the permission of the Magistrate before the registration of the FIR in cases where non-cognizable offences are alleged. The court observed that the provisions of the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 require the permission of the Magistrate for the registration of FIRs in cases where non-cognizable offences are alleged. The court also observed that the provisions of the Karnataka Forest Act, 1963 and the Wild Life Protection Act, 1972 do not override the provisions of the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973.

The court order also emphasizes the need for strict compliance with the provisions of the Karnataka Forest Act, 1963 and the Wild Life Protection Act, 1972. The court observed that the protection of forests and wildlife is of utmost importance and that any contravention of the provisions of the Acts must be dealt with strictly.

Ramakrishna Sherigar against the State of Karnataka highlights the importance of obtaining the permission of the Magistrate before the registration of FIRs in cases where non-cognizable offences are alleged. The court also emphasizes the need for strict compliance with the provisions of the Karnataka Forest Act, 1963 and the Wild Life Protection Act, 1972 for the protection of forests and wildlife.
