The petition was filed against an order passed by the learned Third Additional Sessions Judge Vidisha in Criminal Revision No.135/2014. The case revolves around a disputed transaction involving the purchase of jewelry. The petitioner, Hemant Rajput, had been accused of issuing a cheque in relation to the purchase of jewelry. However, he denied having made any such transaction and claimed that the cheque was issued for a different purpose.

The petitioner had filed an application under Section 45 of the Evidence Act and Section 91 of Cr.P.C. seeking to summon certain documents to prove his innocence. However, the application was rejected by the learned JMFC Vidisha.

The petitioner then filed a petition under Section 482 of Cr.P.C. before the High Court challenging the order passed by the JMFC. The High Court allowed the petition and directed the trial court to afford an opportunity to the petitioner to lead evidence as per the provisions of Section 45 of the Evidence Act.

Section 45 of the Evidence Act deals with the opinion of experts. It provides that when the court has to form an opinion upon a point of foreign law, science or art, or as to identity of handwriting or finger impressions, the opinions upon that point of persons specially skilled in such foreign law, science or art, or in questions as to identity of handwriting or finger impressions, are relevant facts.

The court's decision to allow the petition and direct the trial court to afford an opportunity to the petitioner to lead evidence under Section 45 of the Evidence Act is significant. It indicates that the court is willing to consider expert opinion in order to arrive at a just decision.

The court's decision also highlights the importance of due process and the right to a fair trial. The petitioner had been denied the opportunity to present evidence in his defense, which is a fundamental right guaranteed under the Constitution of India.
