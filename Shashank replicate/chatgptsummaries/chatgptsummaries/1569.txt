The Kerala High Court recently dismissed a writ petition filed by Thankamma Paulose, who had challenged the Pollution Control Board's refusal to renew her application for consent. The court stated that the petitioner could avail of the statutory remedy. 

The case revolved around the Pollution Control Board's decision to refuse to renew Thankamma Paulose's application for consent. The petitioner had argued that the decision was arbitrary and that she had not been given a proper opportunity to be heard. She had also claimed that the decision was in violation of her fundamental rights.

However, the court found that the petitioner had not exhausted the statutory remedy available to her. The court noted that the petitioner could have appealed the decision of the Pollution Control Board to the Appellate Authority under the Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1974. The court stated that the petitioner could not bypass the statutory remedy and approach the court directly.

The court also rejected the petitioner's argument that the decision of the Pollution Control Board was arbitrary. The court noted that the Pollution Control Board had given the petitioner several opportunities to rectify the deficiencies in her application. The court also noted that the petitioner had not produced any evidence to show that the decision was arbitrary.

The court further rejected the petitioner's argument that her fundamental rights had been violated. The court noted that the right to carry on a business or profession was subject to reasonable restrictions in the interest of public health and safety. The court stated that the Pollution Control Board's decision was a reasonable restriction in the interest of public health and safety.

In conclusion, the Kerala High Court dismissed Thankamma Paulose's writ petition challenging the Pollution Control Board's refusal to renew her application for consent. The court held that the petitioner could avail of the statutory remedy available to her and that she could not bypass the statutory remedy and approach the court directly. The court also rejected the petitioner's arguments that the decision of the Pollution Control Board was arbitrary and that her fundamental rights had been violated.
