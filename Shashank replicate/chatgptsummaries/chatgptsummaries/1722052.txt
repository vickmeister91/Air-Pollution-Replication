The Gujarat High Court recently heard an appeal against the acquittal of the accused in a case filed by the Gujarat Pollution Control Board for violating the Air (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1981. The court noted that while it has the power to review and reconsider evidence, it should not interfere with the finding of acquittal unless the lower court's approach is vitiated by manifest illegality or the conclusion is perverse. The court found that the appellant had failed to prove its case beyond reasonable doubt and upheld the acquittal.

The case was filed by the Gujarat Pollution Control Board against the accused for violating the Air (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1981. The trial court had acquitted the accused, and the Gujarat Pollution Control Board had filed an appeal against the acquittal. The appellant argued that the trial court had erred in acquitting the accused and that the evidence on record was sufficient to prove the charges against them.

The Gujarat High Court, while hearing the appeal, noted that it has the power to review and reconsider evidence, but it should not interfere with the finding of acquittal unless the lower court's approach is vitiated by manifest illegality or the conclusion is perverse. The court observed that the trial court had considered the evidence on record and had come to the conclusion that the accused were not guilty of the charges against them. The court further noted that the trial court's findings and conclusion were considered just and proper.

The court order states that the appellant has not provided any evidence to prove their case beyond reasonable doubt, and the trial court was justified in acquitting the respondents of the charges against them. The findings and conclusion of the trial court are considered just and proper, and the appeal is dismissed. Bail bonds, if any, are cancelled.
