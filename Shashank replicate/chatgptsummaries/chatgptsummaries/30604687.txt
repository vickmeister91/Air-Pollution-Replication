The court order also discussed the limitations of the court's jurisdiction during the stage of framing charges in a criminal case. The court can only consider whether a prima facie case has been made and cannot delve deep into the matter for the purpose of appreciating evidence. However, in rare cases, the court may consider defense material if it convincingly demonstrates that the prosecution's version is absurd or concocted. The order also mentions a civil suit filed by the appellant-company against the HAL Housing Committee for not entrusting the development and construction work of the housing project.

In another case, the court discussed a civil dispute between two parties over a housing project. The plaintiff claimed that the defendant violated their agreement by denying them the development and construction work. The defendant argued that the plaintiff failed to acquire the necessary land and violated the terms of the contract. The court determined that the dispute was primarily civil in nature and not a criminal offense of cheating. The court also considered the essential elements of cheating under section 420 of the Indian Penal Code and concluded that there was no evidence of fraudulent or dishonest intention on the part of the defendant.

In yet another case, the appellants accused the respondents of cheating, criminal breach of trust, and forgery. The court found that there was no evidence of fraudulent inducement or willful misrepresentation by the respondents, and therefore, the ingredients of the offense under section 420 of the Indian Penal Code were not attracted. Similarly, the court found that the ingredients of the offense under section 406 of the Indian Penal Code were not attracted as there was no evidence of dishonest misappropriation or disposal of the money by the appellants. Finally, the court found that there was no evidence of forgery committed by the respondents for the purpose of cheating.

In the last case discussed, the appellants were accused of committing various offenses, including fraud and criminal conspiracy. The court found that there was no evidence to support the charges and that the dispute between the parties was primarily civil in nature. The court criticized the investigating officer for acting in a casual and superficial manner and submitting a charge sheet based on unfounded assumptions. The court concluded that there was no prima facie case against the appellants and that the charges against them should be dropped.

In summary, these court orders highlight the importance of evidence and the limitations of the court's jurisdiction during the stage of framing charges in a criminal case. The court must only consider whether a prima facie case has been made and cannot delve deep into the matter for the purpose of appreciating evidence. Additionally, the court must carefully consider the essential elements of the offense before concluding that a criminal offense has been committed. Finally, the court must ensure that the investigating officer has conducted a thorough investigation and submitted a charge sheet based on sound evidence and not unfounded assumptions.
