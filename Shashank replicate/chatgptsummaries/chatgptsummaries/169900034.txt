The petitioner had previously jumped bail but was now in custody since November 17, 2018.

The court considered the petitioner's plea and granted him bail, subject to the satisfaction of the Trial Court. This means that the petitioner will be released from custody once the Trial Court is satisfied with the conditions of his bail.

Section 379 of the Indian Penal Code deals with theft, while Section 120-B deals with criminal conspiracy. The Wild Life Protection Act, 1972, is a legislation aimed at protecting wildlife and preventing their illegal trade and poaching. Sections 29 and 51 of the Act deal with offences related to hunting and poaching of wildlife.

Given the seriousness of the charges against the petitioner, it is likely that the Trial Court will impose strict conditions on his bail. These conditions may include surrendering his passport, reporting to the police regularly, and refraining from contacting certain individuals or visiting certain places.

It is also worth noting that the court's decision to grant bail to the petitioner is not a reflection of his guilt or innocence. The purpose of bail is to ensure that the accused person appears in court for their trial and does not flee from justice. The court must balance the interests of the accused person with the interests of society in ensuring that justice is served.

In this case, the court has determined that the petitioner is not a flight risk and can be released on bail. However, the Trial Court will have the final say on the conditions of his bail and whether he will be allowed to remain free until his trial.

Overall, the court's decision to grant bail to Betal Singh is a reminder of the importance of the bail system in ensuring that justice is served. While the accused person's rights must be protected, the interests of society in preventing crime and punishing offenders must also be taken into account.
