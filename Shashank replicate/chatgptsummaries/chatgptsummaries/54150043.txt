The court order in question pertains to a criminal revision filed by Devki Nandan Dubey and Narendra Awasthi, who were challenging the charges framed against them under Sections 420/34, 467/34, 468/34, 471/34 of IPC. These charges were related to forgery and false signatures on an agreement for the sale of property. The applicants had applied under Section 91 of the Cr.P.C. before the trial court, stating that additional documents sent to the State Examiner were not provided to them. However, the court dismissed the revision, stating that there was sufficient prima facie evidence against the applicants and no illegality or perversity was visible in the impugned order.

The case at hand involves allegations of forgery and false signatures on an agreement for the sale of property. The charges against the applicants were framed under Sections 420/34, 467/34, 468/34, 471/34 of IPC. These sections deal with offences related to cheating, forgery, and using forged documents as genuine. The applicants had filed a criminal revision challenging these charges, stating that additional documents sent to the State Examiner were not provided to them. They had applied under Section 91 of the Cr.P.C., which deals with the production of documents or other things necessary or desirable for the purposes of any investigation, inquiry, trial or other proceeding under the Code.

The court, however, dismissed the revision, stating that there was sufficient prima facie evidence against the applicants. The court observed that the documents sent to the State Examiner were not relevant to the charges framed against the applicants. The court also noted that the applicants had not made any effort to obtain these documents from the State Examiner. The court further observed that the applicants had not challenged the report of the State Examiner, which had found that the signatures on the agreement were forged.

The court also noted that the trial court had considered all the evidence on record and had framed charges against the applicants based on the material available. The court observed that the trial court had applied its mind to the evidence and had found that there was sufficient material to proceed against the applicants. The court further observed that there was no illegality or perversity in the impugned order.
