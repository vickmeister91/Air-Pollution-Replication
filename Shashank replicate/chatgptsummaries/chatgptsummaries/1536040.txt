In a recent court order, the petitioner, who is the owner of an elephant, had applied for permission to transport the elephant to Goa for exhibition purposes. However, the application was rejected on the grounds that the petitioner was involved in two criminal cases related to the elephant. The petitioner then approached the court seeking relief.

After hearing the arguments of both parties, the court quashed the rejection order and directed the petitioner to provide an undertaking to return the elephant to Kerala within six months and comply with the Kerala Captive Elephants Rules. The court also stated that on compliance, the transport permit would be issued as per the Wild Life (Protection) Act.

The court's decision was based on the fact that the petitioner had not been convicted in either of the criminal cases related to the elephant. The court also noted that the petitioner had been granted bail in both cases and was therefore entitled to the presumption of innocence until proven guilty.

The court further observed that the petitioner had complied with all the requirements of the Kerala Captive Elephants Rules and had obtained all the necessary certificates and permissions for the transportation of the elephant. The court also noted that the petitioner had a valid license for the ownership of the elephant.

The court's decision is significant as it upholds the principle of natural justice and the presumption of innocence until proven guilty. The court's decision also recognizes the importance of complying with the rules and regulations governing the ownership and transportation of captive elephants.

The court's decision also highlights the need for a balanced approach in dealing with cases related to captive elephants. While it is important to ensure that the welfare of captive elephants is protected, it is also important to ensure that the rights of the owners of captive elephants are not unduly curtailed.
