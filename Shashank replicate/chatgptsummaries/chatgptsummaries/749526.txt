The court order in question pertains to a dispute between Bihar State Pollution Control and Tata Engineering & Locomotive Co. regarding whether the latter's business of manufacturing locomotives, engines, etc. falls under the category of "metallurgical industry" in the Water Cess Act, 1977. The High Court had ruled in favor of Tata Engineering, stating that it was an engineering industry and not covered under the Act. The Supreme Court upheld the High Court's decision and dismissed the appeal, stating that the Act should be construed strictly and the test of predominant purpose was not established.

The Water Cess Act, 1977, was enacted to provide for the levy and collection of a cess on water consumed by persons carrying on certain industries and by local authorities, with a view to augment the resources of the Central Board and the State Boards for the prevention and control of water pollution. The Act defines "metallurgical industry" as any industry engaged in the production or processing of metals or alloys or any other substances involving the use of any metallurgical process.

In this case, the Bihar State Pollution Control Board had issued a notice to Tata Engineering & Locomotive Co. demanding payment of water cess for the period from 1991 to 1996, on the ground that the company's business of manufacturing locomotives, engines, etc. fell under the category of "metallurgical industry" in the Act. Tata Engineering challenged the notice before the High Court, contending that its business was that of an engineering industry and not a metallurgical industry.

The High Court, after considering the submissions of both parties, held that Tata Engineering's business was that of an engineering industry and not a metallurgical industry. The Court observed that the company's business involved the manufacture of locomotives, engines, etc., which required the use of various engineering processes, such as welding, machining, assembly, etc. The Court further observed that the use of metallurgical processes in the company's business was incidental and not predominant. The Court, therefore, held that Tata Engineering was not liable to pay water cess under the Act.

The Bihar State Pollution Control Board challenged the High Court's decision before the Supreme Court, contending that the High Court had erred in holding that Tata Engineering's business was not a metallurgical industry. The Board argued that the company's business involved the use of various metallurgical processes, such as casting, forging, heat treatment, etc., and, therefore, fell under the category of "metallurgical industry" in the Act.

The Supreme Court, after considering the submissions of both parties, upheld the High Court's decision and dismissed the appeal. The Court observed that the Act should be construed strictly and that the test of predominant purpose was not established in this case. The Court further observed that the use of metallurgical processes in Tata Engineering's business was incidental and not predominant. The Court, therefore, held that Tata Engineering was not liable to pay water cess under the Act.
