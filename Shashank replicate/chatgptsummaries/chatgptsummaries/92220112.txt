The court order in question pertains to a case where the petitioner had filed a petition under section 482 of Cr.P.C. against the order passed by JMFC, Indore in criminal case no. 46060/2006. The petitioner had filed an application under section 91 of Cr.P.C directing the complainant to produce invoices, statements of income tax, sales tax and accounts book to show that the cheque was issued for discharge of any legal liability. However, the trial court rejected the application on the ground that cross-examination of the complainant has already been recorded, in which, the questions have already been put up regarding invoices.

The petitioner had argued that the trial court had erred in rejecting the application under section 91 of Cr.P.C. The petitioner had contended that the complainant had not produced any evidence to show that the cheque was issued for discharge of any legal liability. The petitioner had further argued that the trial court had not considered the fact that the complainant had failed to produce the relevant documents despite being directed to do so.

The court, however, held that the trial court had rightly dismissed the application filed by the petitioner under section 91 of Cr.P.C. The court observed that the trial court had recorded the cross-examination of the complainant, in which, the questions regarding invoices had already been put up. The court noted that the complainant had produced some of the invoices during the cross-examination. The court further observed that the trial court had considered the fact that the complainant had failed to produce some of the invoices and had given reasons for not producing them.

The court held that the trial court had considered all the relevant facts and had given reasons for rejecting the application under section 91 of Cr.P.C. The court further held that no interference under section 482 of Cr.P.C was warranted in the present case. The court observed that section 482 of Cr.P.C. empowers the High Court to exercise its inherent powers to prevent abuse of the process of any court or to secure the ends of justice. However, the court noted that the inherent powers of the High Court cannot be exercised to re-appreciate the evidence or to interfere with the findings of fact recorded by the trial court.
