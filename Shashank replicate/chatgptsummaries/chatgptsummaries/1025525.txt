The court received a Public Interest Litigation (PIL) alleging that a minister of the Mizoram government had illegally killed a barking deer and served its meat at a dinner party. The PIL was filed under the Wild Life (Protection) Act, 1972, which prohibits the hunting of wild animals without a license. The act also prohibits the possession, sale, and transportation of any wild animal or its parts.

An enquiry was conducted by the Mizoram Forest Department, and it was found that the minister had indeed committed an offence under the Wild Life (Protection) Act, 1972. The enquiry report stated that the minister had killed the barking deer without a license and had served its meat at a dinner party. The report also stated that the minister had violated the provisions of the act by possessing and transporting the meat.

However, despite the findings of the enquiry report, the Chief Minister of Mizoram directed the Chief Secretary not to pursue the matter. This decision was criticized by many, including wildlife activists and the petitioner who had filed the PIL. They argued that the Chief Minister's decision was a violation of the rule of law and undermined the authority of the court.

The court, in response to the PIL, directed the Chief Secretary to take appropriate action in accordance with the law within a month. The court's order was seen as a victory for wildlife conservation and the rule of law. It sent a strong message that no one, not even a minister, is above the law.

The Wild Life (Protection) Act, 1972, is a crucial piece of legislation that aims to protect India's rich biodiversity. It provides for the protection of wild animals and plants and regulates their hunting, possession, and transportation. The act also provides for the establishment of national parks, wildlife sanctuaries, and other protected areas.

The act has been instrumental in the conservation of several endangered species, including tigers, elephants, and rhinoceroses. However, the act's implementation has been hampered by several challenges, including poaching, habitat destruction, and human-wildlife conflict.
