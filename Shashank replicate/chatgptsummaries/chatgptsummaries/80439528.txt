The petitioner/accused had raised an objection regarding the production of bank documents during the course of evidence, which was disallowed by the trial court. The accused claimed that they were not given an opportunity to rebut the documents and that the order caused serious prejudice to their rights. However, the court observed that the bank manager was a listed witness in the complaint and had produced documents related to both the petitioner and the complainant. The case in question involves a complaint filed under Section 138 of the Negotiable Instrument Act, which deals with dishonour of cheques. The accused had allegedly issued a cheque to the complainant, which was dishonoured due to insufficient funds. The complainant had then filed a complaint against the accused under Section 138 of the Act. During the course of the trial, the complainant had produced certain bank documents to prove their case. The accused had objected to the production of these documents, claiming that they were not given an opportunity to rebut them.

The trial court had disallowed the objection raised by the accused and allowed the complainant to produce the bank documents as evidence. The accused had then filed a petition under Section 482 Cr.P.C to challenge this order. The accused claimed that they were not given an opportunity to rebut the documents produced by the complainant and that the order caused serious prejudice to their rights.

The court, however, observed that the bank manager was a listed witness in the complaint and had produced documents related to both the petitioner and the complainant. The court noted that the accused had not raised any objection to the examination of the bank manager as a witness. The court also observed that the accused had not cross-examined the bank manager on the documents produced by them. Therefore, the court held that the accused had been given a fair opportunity to rebut the documents produced by the complainant.

The court also noted that the accused had not produced any evidence to rebut the documents produced by the complainant. The court observed that the accused had not produced any evidence to show that the documents produced by the complainant were false or fabricated. Therefore, the court held that the trial court had rightly allowed the complainant to produce the bank documents as evidence.

In conclusion, the court dismissed the petition filed by the accused under Section 482 Cr.P.C to challenge the order passed by the trial court. The court held that the accused had been given a fair opportunity to rebut the documents produced by the complainant and that the trial court had rightly allowed the complainant to produce the bank documents as evidence. The court also observed that the accused had not produced any evidence to rebut the documents produced by the complainant.
