The court order in question pertains to a writ of mandamus filed by a petitioner against the Inspector of Police, who had issued a summons under Section 91 Cr.P.C. asking the petitioner to appear for an enquiry. The petitioner had filed a representation to take action against the police personnel who had registered a false case against him. The petitioner had sought a direction from the court to the 1st respondent to consider the representation as per law within the stipulated time fixed by the court.

The court, however, dismissed the writ petition on the grounds that there was no statutory obligation upon the respondents to dispose of the said representation. The court held that the petitioner had not been able to establish any legal right that entitled him to the relief sought in the writ petition. The court further observed that the issuance of a summons under Section 91 Cr.P.C. did not amount to an infringement of the petitioner's rights.

The court's decision was based on a careful consideration of the facts and circumstances of the case. The court noted that the petitioner had not been able to provide any evidence to support his claim that the police personnel had registered a false case against him. The court also observed that the issuance of a summons under Section 91 Cr.P.C. was a routine procedure that was followed in such cases.

The court's decision was in line with the principles of natural justice and the rule of law. The court held that the respondents were not under any statutory obligation to dispose of the petitioner's representation within a stipulated time. The court further observed that the petitioner had not been able to establish any legal right that entitled him to the relief sought in the writ petition.
