The petitioner, who is the registered owner of the vehicle, sought interim custody of the motorbike through an application under Section 451 of Cr.P.C. The learned Magistrate ordered the release of the vehicle subject to certain conditions, including the furnishing of a bank guarantee for Rs. 80,000.

The case in question involves the seizure of a motorbike in connection with an offense under the Wild Life (Protection) Act, 1972. The petitioner, who is the registered owner of the vehicle, sought interim custody of the motorbike through an application under Section 451 of Cr.P.C. The learned Magistrate ordered the release of the vehicle subject to certain conditions, including the furnishing of a bank guarantee for Rs. 80,000.

The petitioner challenged this condition, arguing that it was unjustified. The court modified the condition and directed the release of the motorbike on interim custody upon the execution of a bond for Rs. 80,000 with two solvent sureties.

The court's decision to modify the condition was based on the fact that the petitioner had already deposited a sum of Rs. 80,000 with the court as security for the release of the motorbike. The court noted that the petitioner had complied with all the conditions imposed by the Magistrate and had also produced all the necessary documents to prove his ownership of the vehicle.

The court further observed that the petitioner had no previous criminal record and was not likely to abscond or tamper with the evidence. The court also took into account the fact that the petitioner had already suffered a great deal of inconvenience and financial loss due to the seizure of the motorbike.

In light of these factors, the court held that the condition of furnishing a bank guarantee for Rs. 80,000 was unjustified and modified it to the execution of a bond for the same amount with two solvent sureties. The court directed the petitioner to produce the motorbike before the court whenever required and to cooperate with the investigating agency in the case.

The court's decision in this case highlights the importance of balancing the interests of justice with the rights of the accused. While it is important to ensure that the accused does not abscond or tamper with the evidence, it is equally important to ensure that the accused's rights are not unduly curtailed.

In this case, the court recognized that the petitioner had already suffered a great deal of inconvenience and financial loss due to the seizure of the motorbike and that the condition of furnishing a bank guarantee for Rs. 80,000 was unjustified. The court's decision to modify the condition and direct the release of the motorbike on interim custody upon the execution of a bond for the same amount with two solvent sureties strikes a balance between the interests of justice and the rights of the accused.
