The Jharkhand High Court recently rejected the request for anticipatory bail by Rajesh Kumar Karmali, who was apprehending his arrest in connection with C.P. Case No. 30 of 2013 for alleged offenses under Sections 37, 38, and 39 of the Air (Prevention & Control of Pollution) Act, 1981. The court found that the factory of the petitioner was found in production despite the closure notice issued by the Pollution Control Board.

The case pertains to the alleged violation of the Air (Prevention & Control of Pollution) Act, 1981, by Rajesh Kumar Karmali, who is the owner of a factory in Jharkhand. The Pollution Control Board had issued a closure notice to the factory, citing non-compliance with the provisions of the Act. However, the factory was found to be in production despite the notice, leading to the filing of a case against Karmali.

Karmali had approached the Jharkhand High Court seeking anticipatory bail, apprehending his arrest in connection with the case. However, the court rejected his plea, citing the seriousness of the offense and the fact that the factory was found to be in production despite the closure notice. The court also noted that the offense was non-bailable and that Karmali had not cooperated with the investigation.

The decision of the Jharkhand High Court is significant as it highlights the importance of compliance with environmental laws and regulations. Non-compliance with the provisions of the Act can lead to serious consequences, including fines, closure of the factory, and imprisonment. The Act also provides for the appointment of inspectors who have the power to enter and inspect any premises where an industry, operation, or process is carried out. The inspectors can also take samples of air, water, or soil for analysis and testing.
