The case in question pertains to a complaint filed under Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881, against the petitioner for dishonour of a cheque. The petitioner had filed a Criminal Misc. Petition against an order passed by the Additional Sessions Judge, which upheld the order passed by the Judicial Magistrate dismissing the petitioner's application seeking to summon a witness to be examined by the accused.

The petitioner had sought to summon a witness to prove that the cheque in question was not issued for the discharge of any debt or liability. However, the court dismissed the petition, stating that the petitioner had already moved a similar application seeking summoning of documents, and as per Section 139 of the Act, the petitioner himself is supposed to rebut the presumption by leading his own evidence.

Section 139 of the Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881, provides for a presumption in favour of the holder of a cheque that it was issued for the discharge of any debt or liability. However, this presumption can be rebutted by the accused by leading evidence to the contrary.

In this case, the court held that the petitioner had failed to lead any evidence to rebut the presumption, and therefore, the trial court was justified in dismissing the application seeking to summon a witness. The court further directed the trial court to finally decide the matter within three months.

The court's decision is in line with the provisions of the Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881, which provide for a presumption in favour of the holder of a cheque. The accused has the burden of proving that the cheque was not issued for the discharge of any debt or liability, and this burden cannot be shifted to the holder of the cheque.
