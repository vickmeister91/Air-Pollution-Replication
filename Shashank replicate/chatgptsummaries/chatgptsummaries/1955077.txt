The case in question is an appeal against a judgment made in STC.No.174 of 1998 on the file of the Judicial Magistrate, Mettupalayam. The complaint was filed under Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act against the accused for drawing a cheque for Rs.3 lakhs to discharge a subsisting liability, which was dishonoured due to insufficient funds. The complainant failed to prove that the cheque was drawn only for discharging a subsisting debt, and the appeal was dismissed. The appellant's application for the production of an additional document was also dismissed.

The Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881, is an act in India that regulates the use of negotiable instruments such as cheques, promissory notes, and bills of exchange. Section 138 of the act deals with the dishonour of cheques for insufficient funds. It states that if a cheque is dishonoured due to insufficient funds, the person who issued the cheque can be held liable and punished with imprisonment for a term that may extend to two years, or with a fine that may extend to twice the amount of the cheque, or with both.

The trial court had examined the evidence presented by both parties and had come to the conclusion that the complainant had failed to prove that the cheque was drawn only for discharging a subsisting debt. The court had also noted that the accused had not denied issuing the cheque, but had only denied that it was issued for the purpose claimed by the complainant. Based on these findings, the trial court had dismissed the complaint.

The complainant had then filed an appeal against this judgment. The appellant had also filed an application for the production of an additional document, which he claimed would support his case.
