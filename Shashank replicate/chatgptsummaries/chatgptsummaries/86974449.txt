The State of Rajasthan filed a series of petitions against the Collector of Bundi and another party regarding the alteration of the boundaries of the Ramgarh Wildlife Sanctuary. The issue at hand was whether the Collector had the power to alter the boundaries, or if the matter was covered by a previous judgment. 

After hearing arguments from both sides, the court ruled in favor of the State, stating that the Collector did not have the power to alter the boundaries. The court ordered the impugned orders to be set aside and remanded the matter to the Collector to reconsider the objections filed by the respondents. 

The Ramgarh Wildlife Sanctuary is a protected area in the state of Rajasthan, India. It is home to a variety of wildlife, including tigers, leopards, and sloth bears. The sanctuary is spread over an area of 252 square kilometers and is an important habitat for many endangered species. 

The issue of altering the boundaries of the sanctuary arose when the Collector of Bundi issued orders to change the boundaries of the sanctuary. The State of Rajasthan filed petitions challenging these orders, arguing that the Collector did not have the power to alter the boundaries. 

The respondents, on the other hand, argued that the Collector did have the power to alter the boundaries and that the matter was not covered by a previous judgment. They contended that the Collector had taken into account the objections raised by the locals and had made the changes to ensure the protection of the wildlife in the sanctuary. 

The court heard arguments from both sides and examined the relevant laws and judgments. After considering all the facts and circumstances of the case, the court ruled in favor of the State. The court held that the Collector did not have the power to alter the boundaries of the sanctuary and that the matter was covered by a previous judgment. 

The court observed that the Collector had not followed the due process of law while altering the boundaries. The court noted that the Collector had not considered the objections raised by the State and had not given any reasons for the changes made. The court held that the Collector's orders were arbitrary and illegal and ordered them to be set aside. 

The court also remanded the matter to the Collector to reconsider the objections filed by the respondents. The court directed the Collector to follow the due process of law and to consider all the objections raised by the respondents before making any changes to the boundaries of the sanctuary.
