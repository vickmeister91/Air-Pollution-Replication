The court order in question pertains to a criminal case where Mathew Dominic is one of the accused. He had filed an application under Section 91 of the Criminal Procedure Code (Cr.P.C.) to obtain call details from telephone companies. However, the trial judge had not acted upon this application. As a result, the petitioner filed an Original Petition under Article 227 of the Constitution of India, seeking directions to the court below to dispose of the application without delay and to direct the telephone companies to preserve the required details.

The court, after hearing the arguments of both parties, allowed the petition and directed the court below to consider and dispose of the application without delay. Additionally, the telephone companies were directed to preserve the required materials until a decision is taken by the court below.

Section 91 of the Cr.P.C. allows a court to issue a summons or order to produce any document or thing that is necessary for the purposes of any investigation, inquiry, trial or other proceeding under the Code. In this case, Mathew Dominic had sought call details from telephone companies, which he believed would be crucial to his defense in the criminal case against him.

However, the trial judge had not acted upon this application, which prompted the petitioner to file an Original Petition under Article 227 of the Constitution of India. Article 227 empowers the High Courts to exercise superintendence over all courts and tribunals throughout the territories in which they have jurisdiction.

The court, after hearing the arguments of both parties, found that the trial judge had not disposed of the application filed by the petitioner under Section 91 of the Cr.P.C. in a timely manner. The court also noted that the call details sought by the petitioner were crucial to his defense in the criminal case against him.

Therefore, the court allowed the petition and directed the court below to consider and dispose of the application without delay. The telephone companies were also directed to preserve the required materials until a decision is taken by the court below.
