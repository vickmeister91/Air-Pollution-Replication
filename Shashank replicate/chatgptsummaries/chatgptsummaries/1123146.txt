The Karnataka High Court recently passed a significant order in multiple petitions filed under Section 482 of the Criminal Procedure Code. The petitions sought to set aside orders passed by the Range Forest Officer, Kudremukh, permitting investigation into cases involving offences under the Karnataka Forest Act, 1963, the Wild Life (Protection) Act, 1972, and the Forest Conservation Act, 1980. The court found that the orders were illegal and improper as the Range Forest Officer cannot be deemed a police officer and the complainant and investigating officer cannot be the same person.

The court allowed the petitions and discharged the accused of the said offences. However, the State is at liberty to proceed against them if there had been commission of the alleged offences. The court's decision is significant as it clarifies the role of the Range Forest Officer and the limitations of their powers.

The court's decision was based on the interpretation of the relevant provisions of the Criminal Procedure Code and the Forest Acts. The court held that the Range Forest Officer is not a police officer and cannot be deemed as such. The court also held that the complainant and investigating officer cannot be the same person as it would violate the principles of natural justice and fairness.
