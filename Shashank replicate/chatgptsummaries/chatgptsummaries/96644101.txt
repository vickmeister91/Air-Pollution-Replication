Gayasuddin Khan, the petitioner, had filed for bail in a case registered under Section 33 of the Indian Forest Act. The petitioner argued that the Notifications dated 26.08.1955 issued on 04.10.1961 annexed with the prosecution report dated 11.12.2008 has lost force after the lapse of 30 years in view of the provisions of Section 30(b) of the Indian Forest Act. However, the State opposed the bail and submitted that the notification dated 04.10.1961 has not lost its force and prohibits certain activities with a view to conserve and regenerate forest for the protection of the environment.

The court, after hearing both sides, directed the petitioner to surrender within two weeks and granted bail on furnishing a bail bond of Rs.10,000/- with two sureties of the like amount each. The court's decision was based on the fact that the petitioner had been in custody for over a year, and there was no evidence to suggest that he would abscond or tamper with evidence if released on bail.

The case highlights the importance of the Indian Forest Act in protecting the environment and conserving forests. The Act provides for the protection of forests and wildlife, and the prevention of illegal felling of trees, hunting, and poaching. The notification dated 04.10.1961, which the State argued had not lost its force, prohibits certain activities that could harm the environment and the forests.

The court's decision to grant bail to the petitioner was based on the principle of justice and fairness. The court recognized that the petitioner had been in custody for over a year and that there was no evidence to suggest that he would abscond or tamper with evidence if released on bail. The court also recognized the importance of protecting the environment and conserving forests, which are essential for the survival of all living beings.
