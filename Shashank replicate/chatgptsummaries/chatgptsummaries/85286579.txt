The court order in question pertains to a bail application filed under Section 439 of the Criminal Procedure Code (Cr.P.C.) for an offence punishable under Sections 9 and 51 of the Wild Life Protection Act, 1972. The applicant in this case was apprehended and a snake was seized from his possession. The applicant's counsel argued that he had not committed any offence and had been falsely implicated.

The court heard the arguments put forth by the applicant's counsel and considered the evidence presented before it. After careful consideration, the court allowed the bail application and directed the applicant to be released on bail upon his furnishing a personal bond in the sum of Rs.50,000/- with certain conditions to be complied with.

The Wild Life Protection Act, 1972, is a comprehensive legislation that seeks to protect and conserve wildlife in India. The Act provides for the establishment of national parks, wildlife sanctuaries, and other protected areas, and lays down stringent penalties for offences related to the hunting, poaching, and trade of wildlife.

Sections 9 and 51 of the Act deal with offences related to hunting and poaching of wildlife. Section 9 prohibits the hunting of any wild animal specified in Schedule I, II, III, and IV of the Act, while Section 51 provides for punishment for offences under the Act.

In this case, the applicant was charged with an offence under Section 9 of the Act for allegedly hunting a snake. The prosecution had presented evidence to support their case, including the seizure of the snake from the applicant's possession.

However, the applicant's counsel argued that their client had not committed any offence and had been falsely implicated. They pointed out that there was no evidence to suggest that the applicant had hunted the snake, and that it was possible that the snake had been found in his possession by chance.

The court considered the arguments put forth by both sides and examined the evidence presented before it. After careful consideration, the court found that there was no conclusive evidence to suggest that the applicant had hunted the snake. The court also noted that the applicant had no prior criminal record and was not likely to abscond or tamper with evidence.

Taking these factors into account, the court allowed the bail application and directed the applicant to be released on bail upon his furnishing a personal bond in the sum of Rs.50,000/- with certain conditions to be complied with. The conditions included that the applicant would not leave the jurisdiction of the court without prior permission, would not tamper with evidence, and would cooperate with the investigation.

In conclusion, the court order in question pertains to a bail application filed under Section 439 of the Cr.P.C. for an offence punishable under Sections 9 and 51 of the Wild Life Protection Act, 1972. The court allowed the bail application and directed the applicant to be released on bail upon his furnishing a personal bond in the sum of Rs.50,000/- with certain conditions to be complied with. The court found that there was no conclusive evidence to suggest that the applicant had hunted the snake and that he was not likely to abscond or tamper with evidence.
