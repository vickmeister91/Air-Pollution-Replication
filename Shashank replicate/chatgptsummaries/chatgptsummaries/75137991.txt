The court order in question pertains to a petition filed by Vinod Singh under Section 482 of the Cr.P.C. against an order passed by the Twelfth Additional Sessions Judge, Bhopal in Criminal Revision No.105/14 Vinod Singh Vs. Raj Kumar. The petitioner had filed an application under Section 91 of the Cr.P.C. to call for documents from the Court of Tehsildar, Tehsil Hujur, district Bhopal, which was disallowed by the learned JMFC. Feeling aggrieved, the petitioner filed a revision which was dismissed by the learned ASJ.

The petitioner, Vinod Singh, had filed an application under Section 91 of the Cr.P.C. to call for documents from the Court of Tehsildar, Tehsil Hujur, district Bhopal. However, this application was disallowed by the learned JMFC. The petitioner then filed a revision against this order, which was dismissed by the learned ASJ. The petitioner then approached the High Court under Section 482 of the Cr.P.C.

The High Court, after hearing the arguments of both parties, held that the petitioner may get certified copies of the documents he wants to file in support of his defense. The court also observed that the petitioner had not made out a case for interference under Section 482 of the Cr.P.C. The court further held that the petitioner had an alternative remedy available to him under Section 205 of the Cr.P.C., which he could have availed of.

The court's decision in this case is significant as it clarifies the scope of Section 91 of the Cr.P.C. Section 91 empowers a court to issue a summons or order to produce a document or thing if it is necessary or desirable for the purposes of any investigation, inquiry, trial or other proceeding under the Cr.P.C. However, the court's decision in this case makes it clear that a person seeking to obtain documents under Section 91 must make out a case for their relevance and necessity.

The court's decision also highlights the importance of alternative remedies available to litigants. In this case, the court observed that the petitioner had an alternative remedy available to him under Section 205 of the Cr.P.C. This section empowers a court to dispense with the personal appearance of an accused and allow him to appear through his lawyer. The court's observation in this regard is a reminder to litigants that they must exhaust all available remedies before approaching the High Court under Section 482 of the Cr.P.C.

In conclusion, the court's decision in this case clarifies the scope of Section 91 of the Cr.P.C. and highlights the importance of alternative remedies available to litigants. The court's decision also serves as a reminder to litigants that they must make out a case for interference under Section 482 of the Cr.P.C. and exhaust all available remedies before approaching the High Court.
