The Gujarat High Court recently quashed and set aside a criminal case filed against Ballarpur Industries Ltd. and 10 others for alleged breach of the Water (Prevention & Control of Pollution) Act, 1974. The company had filed an application under Section 482 of the Code of Criminal Procedure for quashing the case, arguing that the powers delegated to the complainant were not legal and proper. The court agreed with the company's argument and ruled that the complaint and process issued therein were required to be quashed and set aside.

The Water (Prevention & Control of Pollution) Act, 1974 is a crucial piece of legislation that aims to prevent and control water pollution in India. The Act empowers the Central and State Pollution Control Boards to take measures to prevent and control water pollution. It also provides for the establishment of pollution control committees at the district and local levels.

In this case, Ballarpur Industries Ltd. and 10 others were accused of violating the provisions of the Act. The complainant, who was a member of the pollution control committee, had filed a complaint against the accused. However, the accused argued that the powers delegated to the complainant were not legal and proper, and therefore, the complaint and process issued therein were required to be quashed and set aside.

The court, after considering the arguments put forth by both parties, agreed with the accused and quashed the case. The court held that the complainant had not been properly authorized to file the complaint and that the process issued therein was not legal and proper. The court also observed that the complainant had not followed the proper procedure for filing the complaint and had not obtained the necessary approvals from the pollution control committee.

The court's decision in this case is significant as it highlights the importance of following the proper procedure while filing complaints under the Water (Prevention & Control of Pollution) Act, 1974. The Act provides for a detailed procedure for filing complaints, and it is essential to follow this procedure to ensure that the complaint is legally valid and can be acted upon.

The court's decision also emphasizes the need for proper delegation of powers while appointing members to pollution control committees. The Act provides for the appointment of members to these committees, and it is essential to ensure that the members appointed have the necessary qualifications and are authorized to carry out their duties.

In conclusion, the Gujarat High Court's decision to quash the criminal case filed against Ballarpur Industries Ltd. and 10 others for alleged breach of the Water (Prevention & Control of Pollution) Act, 1974 highlights the importance of following the proper procedure while filing complaints under the Act. The decision also emphasizes the need for proper delegation of powers while appointing members to pollution control committees. It is essential to ensure that the members appointed have the necessary qualifications and are authorized to carry out their duties to prevent and control water pollution in India.
