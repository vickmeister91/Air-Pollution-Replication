The Indian judiciary has been instrumental in protecting the country's natural resources and environment. In a recent case, the court dismissed a public interest litigation (PIL) seeking to quash a tender notice for mining in a reserved forest area. The petitioners argued that forest land cannot be used for non-forest activities under the Forest (Conservation) Act, 1980. However, the court found that the state government can use forest land for non-forest purposes with prior approval from the central government, which was obtained in this case. Therefore, the court found no illegality in the tender notice and dismissed the petition.

The Forest (Conservation) Act, 1980, was enacted to regulate the diversion of forest land for non-forest purposes. The act requires prior approval from the central government for any diversion of forest land. The act also provides for the constitution of a National Board for Wildlife to advise the central government on matters relating to the conservation of wildlife and forests.

In the present case, the state government had issued a tender notice for mining in a reserved forest area. The petitioners, who were environmental activists, challenged the tender notice on the grounds that it violated the Forest (Conservation) Act, 1980. They argued that forest land cannot be used for non-forest activities, and the state government had not obtained prior approval from the central government for the diversion of forest land.

The court examined the provisions of the Forest (Conservation) Act, 1980, and observed that the act does not prohibit the use of forest land for non-forest purposes. The act only requires prior approval from the central government for any diversion of forest land. The court noted that the state government had obtained prior approval from the central government for the diversion of forest land for mining purposes. Therefore, the court found no illegality in the tender notice.

The court also observed that the state government had taken adequate measures to mitigate the environmental impact of mining in the reserved forest area. The state government had imposed several conditions on the mining company, such as the payment of compensation for the loss of forest land, the implementation of an afforestation program, and the creation of a wildlife corridor. The court noted that these conditions were aimed at minimizing the adverse impact of mining on the environment and wildlife.

The court's decision in this case is significant as it clarifies the scope of the Forest (Conservation) Act, 1980.
