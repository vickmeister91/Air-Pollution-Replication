The High Court of Judicature at Madras recently dismissed a revision petition filed by an accused in a dishonour of cheque case. The petitioner had filed petitions seeking the production of certain documents related to a chit transaction, which the respondent had opposed, stating that the documents were not necessary for deciding the case. The XVIII Metropolitan Magistrate, Saidapet, Chennai, had dismissed the petitions, and the petitioner had filed revisions against the order.

The accused had been charged with dishonour of cheque under Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881. The petitioner had filed petitions seeking the production of certain documents related to a chit transaction, which he claimed were necessary for his defence. The respondent had opposed the petitions, stating that the documents were not relevant to the case and were being sought only to delay the proceedings.

The XVIII Metropolitan Magistrate, Saidapet, Chennai, had dismissed the petitions, stating that the documents sought by the accused were not relevant for deciding the dishonour of cheque case. The petitioner had then filed revisions against the order in the High Court of Judicature at Madras.

The High Court of Judicature at Madras dismissed the revisions, stating that the documents sought by the accused were not relevant for deciding the dishonour of cheque case. The court observed that the accused had not made out a case for the production of the documents and that the documents were being sought only to delay the proceedings.

The court also observed that the accused had not made out a case for the production of the documents under Section 91 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973. Section 91 empowers a court to issue a summons or order for the production of any document or other thing that is necessary or desirable for the purposes of any investigation, inquiry, trial or other proceeding under the Code.

The court held that the accused had not shown that the documents sought by him were necessary or desirable for the purposes of the trial. The court also observed that the accused had not shown that the documents were in the possession of the respondent or any other person who could be compelled to produce them.

The court further observed that the accused had not shown that the documents were relevant to the case. The court held that the accused had not made out a case for the production of the documents and that the revisions were liable to be dismissed.

In conclusion, the High Court of Judicature at Madras dismissed the revisions filed by the accused seeking the production of certain documents related to a chit transaction.
