The Karnataka High Court recently issued a court order in response to a writ petition filed by Wildlife First against the State of Karnataka. The petition sought a mandamus to prevent commercial activities within the Kudremukh National Park. The court has directed the Additional Chief Secretary, Forest, Ecology and Environment Department, Government of Karnataka, to consider the matter in the background of the Wild Life (Protection) Act, 1972. The government has been given four months to pass a reasoned and speaking order after giving opportunities of hearing to all concerned in the matter. Any action taken in the meantime shall abide by the result of the decision taken by the government. The writ petition stands disposed of with no order as to costs.

The Kudremukh National Park is a protected area in the Western Ghats of Karnataka, India. It is home to a variety of flora and fauna, including endangered species such as the lion-tailed macaque, the Malabar giant squirrel, and the Indian wild dog. The park is also a major source of water for the region, with three major rivers originating from its hills.

The writ petition filed by Wildlife First was in response to concerns about commercial activities within the park. The petition argued that such activities were in violation of the Wild Life (Protection) Act, 1972, which prohibits any activity that is detrimental to the conservation of wildlife. The petition also argued that the activities were causing damage to the park's ecosystem and threatening the survival of endangered species.

The court order directs the government to consider the matter in light of the Wild Life (Protection) Act, 1972. This act provides for the protection of wild animals and plants and their habitats. It also prohibits hunting, poaching, and other activities that are detrimental to the conservation of wildlife. The act also provides for the establishment of national parks, wildlife sanctuaries, and other protected areas for the conservation of wildlife.

The court has given the government four months to pass a reasoned and speaking order after giving opportunities of hearing to all concerned in the matter. This means that the government must consider the views of all stakeholders, including environmentalists, local communities, and businesses that may be affected by the decision. The order also states that any action taken in the meantime shall abide by the result of the decision taken by the government. This means that any commercial activities within the park must be suspended until the government makes a decision.
