In a recent court order, the release of a vehicle seized for an offence committed under the Wild Life (Protection) Act, 1972 was challenged by the petitioner. The lower courts had denied the release of the vehicle, but the court held that the vehicle does not automatically become the property of the state upon seizure and can be released if a competent court of law does not find that the vehicle was used for the commission of the offence.

The court also held that the magistrate has the power to release the vehicle under Section 457 of the Cr. P.C. This section empowers the magistrate to order the release of any property seized under the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, if he is satisfied that it is no longer required for the purpose of investigation or trial.

The court ordered the release of the vehicle subject to certain conditions. These conditions included the payment of a bond amount and the furnishing of an undertaking by the petitioner that the vehicle will be produced before the court whenever required. The court also directed the petitioner to cooperate with the investigation and to attend the court proceedings as and when required.
