In a recent court order, Mohsim Ali was convicted for violating the Wildlife (Protection) Act, 1972 by possessing wild birds without proper authorization. The prosecution presented evidence that Ali was found in possession of 3 Blossom headed parakeets and 200 Red Munias without any documents to prove lawful possession. The court sentenced Ali to three years of rigorous imprisonment and a fine of Rs 25,000. This was not Ali's first offense, as he had previously been convicted for similar offenses and sentenced to imprisonment and fines.

The Wildlife (Protection) Act, 1972 is a comprehensive legislation that provides for the protection of wild animals and plants and for matters connected therewith or ancillary or incidental thereto. The Act prohibits hunting, poaching, capturing, and killing of wild animals and birds, and also regulates the trade in wildlife and their products. The Act also provides for the establishment of national parks, sanctuaries, and other protected areas for the conservation of wildlife.

In this case, the court found that Ali had violated the provisions of the Act by possessing wild birds without proper authorization. The court noted that the possession of wild birds without proper authorization is a serious offense that can have a detrimental impact on the conservation of wildlife. The court also observed that Ali had been previously convicted for similar offenses, which indicated a disregard for the law and the need for deterrence.

The court sentenced Ali to three years of rigorous imprisonment and a fine of Rs 25,000. The court also ordered the confiscation and destruction of the birds that were found in Ali's possession. The court noted that the confiscation and destruction of the birds was necessary to prevent their illegal trade and to deter others from engaging in similar offenses. The court also consigned the case file to the record room.
