The case at hand involves a dispute between Mr. K.C. Chandrashekar Raju and Max Hypermarket India Pvt Ltd. The petitioner had filed a criminal petition seeking direction to DW-3 to produce certain documents during the trial of a prosecution launched for the offence under Section 138 of the Negotiable Instrument Act. The court dismissed the application, stating that the documents were not necessary for the proper decision in the matter.

The petitioner challenged the order, arguing that the documents were crucial to the case and that the court below had erred in not exercising the discretion vested under Section 91 of Cr.P.C. The court, however, rejected the petition, stating that the court below was justified in not exercising the discretion vested under Section 91 of Cr.P.C.

Section 91 of Cr.P.C. empowers a court to issue a summons or order to a person to produce a document or thing that is necessary or desirable for the purposes of any investigation, inquiry, trial or other proceeding under the Code. The section vests a discretionary power in the court to issue such summons or order, and the court may refuse to exercise this discretion if it is of the opinion that the document or thing is not necessary or desirable for the purposes of the proceeding.

In the present case, the court found that the documents sought by the petitioner were not necessary for the proper decision in the matter. The court noted that the petitioner had failed to establish the relevance of the documents to the case and that the documents did not appear to be directly connected to the offence under Section 138 of the Negotiable Instrument Act.

The court also observed that the petitioner had not made any effort to obtain the documents through other means, such as by issuing a notice under Section 91 of Cr.P.C. to the person in possession of the documents. The court noted that the petitioner had merely relied on the fact that DW-3 was a witness in the case and had assumed that the documents would be in his possession.

The court, therefore, held that the court below was justified in not exercising the discretion vested under Section 91 of Cr.P.C. and in dismissing the petitioner's application. The court also noted that the petitioner had failed to establish any ground for interference with the order of the court below.
