The Madras High Court recently passed a ruling on a writ petition filed by ST CMS Co. against the Tamil Nadu Pollution Control Board, the District Environmental Engineer, the District Collector, the Chairman & Managing Director of Neyveli Lignite Corporation, and ST-CMS Electric Company. The petitioner sought the revocation of the consent order given to the 5th respondent under the T.N. Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act of 1974 and the decommissioning of their Thermal Power Project. 

The court observed that the petitioner had an effective and viable remedy of preferring an appeal before the Appellate Authority/Pollution Control Board, Chennai. The court further noted that the petitioner had not exhausted this remedy before approaching the court. Therefore, the court disposed of the writ petition, stating that the petitioner must first exhaust the available remedy before approaching the court.

The case highlights the importance of exhausting all available remedies before approaching the court. The court's ruling emphasizes that the judiciary is not the first port of call for resolving disputes. Instead, parties must first explore all available administrative remedies before approaching the court.

The T.N. Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act of 1974 provides for the prevention and control of water pollution and the maintaining or restoring of wholesomeness of water. The Act empowers the Pollution Control Board to grant consent to establish and operate industries that may discharge effluents into water bodies. The Act also provides for the revocation of such consent orders if the industry violates the conditions of the consent order or causes pollution.

In this case, the petitioner sought the revocation of the consent order given to the 5th respondent under the Act. The petitioner also sought the decommissioning of the Thermal Power Project. The court observed that the petitioner had not exhausted the available remedy of preferring an appeal before the Appellate Authority/Pollution Control Board, Chennai. The court noted that the petitioner had not provided any reasons for not preferring an appeal before the Appellate Authority/Pollution Control Board, Chennai.

The court's ruling highlights the importance of following due process and exhausting all available remedies before approaching the court. The court's decision also emphasizes the role of administrative bodies in resolving disputes. Administrative bodies such as the Pollution Control Board play a crucial role in enforcing environmental laws and regulations. Therefore, parties must first approach these bodies before approaching the court.

The case also highlights the need for industries to comply with environmental laws and regulations. The T.N. Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act of 1974 provides for the prevention and control of water pollution. Industries must comply with the conditions of the consent order granted by the Pollution Control Board. Failure to comply with these conditions can result in the revocation of the consent order and the closure of the industry.

In conclusion, the Madras High Court's ruling on the writ petition filed by ST CMS Co. highlights the importance of exhausting all available remedies before approaching the court. The court's decision emphasizes the role of administrative bodies in resolving disputes and enforcing environmental laws and regulations. The case also highlights the need for industries to comply with environmental laws and regulations to prevent pollution and protect the environment.
