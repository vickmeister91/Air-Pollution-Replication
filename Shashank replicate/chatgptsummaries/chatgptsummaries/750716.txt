The court order pertains to a criminal appeal against the acquittal of a factory and its partners for discharging untreated water into a river. The trial court had acquitted the accused due to lack of evidence and failure to follow mandatory rules. The appellate court found no reason to interfere with the trial court's decision and dismissed the appeal, confirming the acquittal.

The case involves a factory and its partners who were accused of discharging dirty water without purifying it and passing it into a river. The trial court had acquitted the accused due to lack of evidence and failure to follow mandatory rules. The prosecution had argued that the accused had violated the Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1974, and the Environment (Protection) Act, 1986, by discharging untreated water into the river.

The trial court had observed that the prosecution had failed to prove that the accused had discharged untreated water into the river. The court had also noted that the prosecution had not followed the mandatory rules for collecting and preserving the water samples. The court had further observed that the prosecution had not produced any evidence to show that the accused had violated the provisions of the Water Act and the Environment Act.

The prosecution had filed an appeal against the acquittal of the accused. The appellate court heard the appeal and examined the evidence on record. The court noted that the prosecution had failed to prove that the accused had discharged untreated water into the river. The court also observed that the prosecution had not followed the mandatory rules for collecting and preserving the water samples.

The appellate court found no reason to interfere with the trial court's decision and dismissed the appeal, confirming the acquittal. The court held that the prosecution had failed to prove the guilt of the accused beyond reasonable doubt. The court also observed that the prosecution had not followed the mandatory rules for collecting and preserving the water samples.
