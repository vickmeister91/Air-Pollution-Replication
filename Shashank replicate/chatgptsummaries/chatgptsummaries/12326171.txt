In a recent court order, three accused persons who were convicted under the Wild Life (Protection) Act filed a criminal revision petition challenging their conviction. The petitioners argued that there was no cogent evidence to connect them to the crime and that the forest officials' testimony was biased. The court found that there was no satisfactory ocular evidence and that the prosecution had not examined any independent witnesses. As a result, the court allowed the revision petition and acquitted the accused.

The Wild Life (Protection) Act is a law in India that provides for the protection of wild animals and plants and for matters connected therewith or ancillary or incidental thereto. The act was enacted in 1972 and has been amended several times since then. The act provides for the establishment of national parks, wildlife sanctuaries, and conservation reserves for the protection of wildlife.

In this case, the accused persons were charged with hunting a wild animal without a license and possessing the animal's skin. The prosecution relied on the testimony of forest officials who claimed to have seen the accused persons hunting the animal and possessing its skin. However, the accused persons denied the charges and claimed that they were falsely implicated.

The court examined the evidence presented by the prosecution and found that there was no satisfactory ocular evidence to connect the accused persons to the crime. The court noted that the forest officials' testimony was biased and that they had a vested interest in securing the conviction of the accused persons. The court also observed that the prosecution had not examined any independent witnesses to corroborate the forest officials' testimony.

Based on these findings, the court allowed the revision petition and acquitted the accused persons. The court held that the prosecution had failed to prove the charges against the accused persons beyond a reasonable doubt. The court also observed that the accused persons had been in custody for a considerable period and that their acquittal was long overdue.

The court's order in this case highlights the importance of ensuring that the prosecution presents cogent and reliable evidence to prove the charges against the accused persons. The court's observation that the forest officials' testimony was biased underscores the need for impartial and independent witnesses to corroborate the prosecution's case.
