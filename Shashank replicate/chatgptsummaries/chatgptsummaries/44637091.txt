Abdul Rashid had filed a Criminal Revision under Section 397 of Cr.P.C. against the order passed by the Special Judge (NDPS Act), Dewas in S.S.T.No.3/2017. The order dismissed his application filed under Section 91 of the Cr.P.C. The High Court of Madhya Pradesh dismissed the revision petition as the impugned order was an interlocutory order, and therefore, not maintainable.

The case pertains to Abdul Rashid, who had filed an application under Section 91 of the Cr.P.C. before the Special Judge (NDPS Act), Dewas. The application sought the production of certain documents from the prosecution. The Special Judge dismissed the application, and Abdul Rashid filed a Criminal Revision under Section 397 of Cr.P.C. before the High Court of Madhya Pradesh.

The High Court of Madhya Pradesh dismissed the revision petition filed by Abdul Rashid. The court held that the impugned order was an interlocutory order, and therefore, not maintainable. The court relied on the decision of the Supreme Court in the case of State of Maharashtra v. Praful Desai, (2003) 4 SCC 601, where it was held that an interlocutory order cannot be challenged under Section 397 of Cr.P.C.

The court further observed that the order passed by the Special Judge was not a final order, and therefore, Abdul Rashid had an alternative remedy available to him. The court noted that Abdul Rashid could challenge the order passed by the Special Judge at the time of the final disposal of the case. The court also observed that the order passed by the Special Judge did not cause any irreparable loss or injury to Abdul Rashid.

The court also referred to the decision of the Supreme Court in the case of State of Punjab v. Davinder Pal Singh Bhullar, (2011) 14 SCC 770, where it was held that an interlocutory order can be challenged only if it causes irreparable loss or injury to the party. The court noted that Abdul Rashid had not made any such claim in his revision petition.

In conclusion, the High Court of Madhya Pradesh dismissed the revision petition filed by Abdul Rashid as the impugned order was an interlocutory order, and therefore, not maintainable. The court held that Abdul Rashid had an alternative remedy available to him, and he could challenge the order passed by the Special Judge at the time of the final disposal of the case. The court also observed that the order passed by the Special Judge did not cause any irreparable loss or injury to Abdul Rashid.
