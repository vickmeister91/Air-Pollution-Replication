The court order in question pertains to a petition filed by Nawab Chandela, who challenged the dismissal of his application under Section 91 of the Criminal Procedure Code (Cr.P.C.). The application sought to bring on record a CD containing a conversation of the victim with some individuals, allegedly claiming that the First Information Report (FIR) lodged by her was false. The court noted that the CD was made available to the Investigating Officer during the investigation but was not placed on record. The court found no illegality or irregularity in the impugned order and dismissed the petition.

The case revolves around the admissibility of a CD containing a conversation between the victim and some individuals, which allegedly proves that the FIR lodged by her was false. The petitioner, Nawab Chandela, sought to bring this CD on record by filing an application under Section 91 of the Cr.P.C. However, the application was dismissed by the court, and the petitioner challenged this dismissal in a petition.

The court noted that the CD was made available to the Investigating Officer during the investigation but was not placed on record. The court also observed that the petitioner had not explained why the CD was not produced before the court during the trial. The court further noted that the petitioner had not challenged the admissibility of the CD before the trial court, and therefore, the issue of admissibility could not be raised before the court in the present petition.

The court also observed that the petitioner had not challenged the impugned order on the ground of any illegality or irregularity. The court noted that the impugned order was passed after considering the relevant provisions of the Cr.P.C. and the evidence on record. The court found no illegality or irregularity in the impugned order and dismissed the petition.

The court's decision in this case highlights the importance of following the proper legal procedures while presenting evidence in court. The petitioner in this case failed to produce the CD before the trial court and did not challenge its admissibility at the appropriate time. As a result, the court found no grounds to allow the CD to be brought on record.

Furthermore, the court's decision emphasizes the need for parties to challenge court orders on specific grounds of illegality or irregularity. In this case, the petitioner failed to challenge the impugned order on any such grounds, and the court found no reason to interfere with the order.
