The Madras High Court recently heard a criminal revision filed by M/S.Arvind Remedies Limited and Dr.B.Arvind Shah against M/s."Voice of Nature" and others under Section 25 of the Tamil Nadu Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1974. The petitioners sought discharge, claiming that the first respondent had no locus standi to file a private complaint against them and that the allegations made did not constitute an offense. The court heard arguments from both sides and reserved judgment.

The court order states that the petitioners have been accused of committing an offense under Section 25 of the Act and must face trial. The court has dismissed the petition seeking discharge and directed the court below to proceed further in accordance with the law. The personal appearance of the petitioner is dispensed with, but they must appear before the court when required. The first respondent has locus standi to file a complaint against the petitioners, and the court has to see the allegations made in the complaint and the documents annexed with the complaint while deciding the petition seeking discharge.

The case revolves around allegations of pollution and violation of the Tamil Nadu Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1974. The first respondent, "Voice of Nature," filed a private complaint against M/S.Arvind Remedies Limited and Dr.B.Arvind Shah, alleging that they had violated the provisions of the Act by discharging untreated effluent into the environment. The petitioners, on the other hand, claimed that the first respondent had no locus standi to file the complaint and that the allegations made did not constitute an offense.

During the hearing, the court heard arguments from both sides and reserved judgment. In its order, the court stated that the petitioners had been accused of committing an offense under Section 25 of the Act and must face trial. The court dismissed the petition seeking discharge and directed the court below to proceed further in accordance with the law. The personal appearance of the petitioner is dispensed with, but they must appear before the court when required.

The court also held that the first respondent had locus standi to file a complaint against the petitioners. The court emphasized that the allegations made in the complaint and the documents annexed with the complaint must be considered while deciding the petition seeking discharge. This means that the court will have to examine the evidence presented by the first respondent and determine whether it is sufficient to establish the guilt of the petitioners.
