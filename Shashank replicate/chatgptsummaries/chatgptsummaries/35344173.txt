The court order in question pertains to an application filed under Section 482 of the Criminal Procedure Code (CrPC) against an order passed by the Additional Sessions Judge (ASJ) in Karera, District Shivpuri, in Criminal Revision No. 143/2017. The ASJ had affirmed the order passed by the Judicial Magistrate First Class (JMFC) in Karera, District Shivpuri, in POR No. 73/2011. The case involved the illegal transportation of sand and an offence under Sections 2/12, 18, 27, 29, 39(d), 50 and 51 of the Wildlife (Protection) Act.

The applicant's tractor was found to be illegally transporting sand and was seized. The applicant filed an application for the release of the tractor, which was rejected by the Magistrate. The applicant challenged the orders passed by the Courts below, contending that the guilt of the applicant had not been established in the trial and until and unless a person is held guilty for committing the offence, the vehicle cannot be confiscated.

The court rejected this contention and held that separate proceedings have been prescribed for confiscation of the vehicle, and the trial and conviction of the accused is not a sine qua non for confiscation of the vehicle. The court also held that the applicant had not produced any royalty receipt to show that he was transporting the sand in accordance with the law. The court noted that the menace of illegal excavation of sand is increasing day by day in the locality and therefore, it is required to be dealt with firmly.

The court affirmed the orders passed by the Courts below, rejecting the application filed under Sections 451 and 457 of the CrPC. The court's decision was based on the fact that the applicant had not produced any evidence to show that he was transporting the sand in accordance with the law. The court also noted that the illegal excavation of sand is a growing problem in the locality and needs to be dealt with firmly.

The court's decision is significant because it clarifies that the trial and conviction of the accused is not a sine qua non for the confiscation of the vehicle. The court's decision also highlights the importance of producing evidence to show that the sand is being transported in accordance with the law. The court's decision is likely to have a significant impact on cases involving the illegal transportation of sand and other natural resources.
