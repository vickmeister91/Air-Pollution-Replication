In a recent court order, a petition filed by B. Vanithamani under Article 226 of the Constitution of India for the issuance of a writ of Habeas Corpus directing the respondents to secure R. The court heard the arguments of both parties and found that Balaji was already in judicial custody, so the petition was closed as there was nothing left to adjudicate.

The writ of Habeas Corpus is a legal action that requires a person who is being detained or imprisoned to be brought before a court or judge. The purpose of this writ is to ensure that the detention is lawful and that the person is not being held in illegal custody. In this case, Vanithamani had filed a petition seeking the issuance of a writ of Habeas Corpus to secure the release of Balaji from illegal custody.

The court heard the arguments of both parties and found that Balaji was already in judicial custody. Judicial custody refers to the detention of a person in a prison or jail, pending trial or investigation. It is different from police custody, which is the detention of a person by the police for questioning or investigation. The court found that since Balaji was already in judicial custody, there was no need for the writ of Habeas Corpus to be issued.

The court's decision to close the petition was based on the fact that there was nothing left to adjudicate. Adjudication refers to the legal process of resolving a dispute or controversy. In this case, since Balaji was already in judicial custody, there was no dispute or controversy to be resolved. The court's decision to close the petition was therefore based on the fact that there was no legal basis for the petition to continue.
