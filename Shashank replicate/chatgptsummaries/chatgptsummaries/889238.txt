The court order in question pertains to a criminal appeal filed by a law officer against Ambuja Foods Ltd. and seven others for violating the Water (Prevention of Control of Pollution) Act, 1974. The accused were acquitted by the learned Judicial Magistrate, First Class, Kadi, and the appellant filed an appeal against the judgment. The High Court of Gujarat at Ahmedabad heard the appeal and dismissed it, confirming the acquittal of the accused.

The case revolves around the alleged violation of the Water (Prevention of Control of Pollution) Act, 1974 by Ambuja Foods Ltd. and seven others. The prosecution had alleged that the accused had discharged untreated effluent into a nearby water body, thereby causing pollution. The case was heard by the Judicial Magistrate, First Class, Kadi, who acquitted the accused on the grounds that the prosecution had failed to prove its case beyond reasonable doubt.

The law officer, who was the appellant in this case, filed an appeal against the judgment of the Judicial Magistrate, First Class, Kadi. The appeal was heard by the High Court of Gujarat at Ahmedabad. The court found that the prosecution had failed to prove its case beyond reasonable doubt, and therefore, the accused were entitled to the benefit of doubt. The court also noted that the evidence presented by the prosecution was not sufficient to establish the guilt of the accused.

The court observed that the prosecution had relied on the testimony of a single witness, who was an employee of the Gujarat Pollution Control Board. The witness had stated that he had collected samples of the effluent discharged by the accused and had found that it was not in compliance with the prescribed standards. However, the court noted that the witness had not followed the prescribed procedure for collecting the samples, and therefore, the samples could not be relied upon.

The court also observed that the prosecution had failed to establish a clear link between the effluent discharged by the accused and the pollution of the nearby water body. The court noted that there were other sources of pollution in the vicinity, and it was not clear whether the pollution was caused by the accused or by these other sources.

In light of these observations, the court dismissed the appeal filed by the law officer and confirmed the acquittal of the accused. The court held that the prosecution had failed to prove its case beyond reasonable doubt, and therefore, the accused were entitled to the benefit of doubt.
