The Kerala High Court recently dismissed a writ petition filed by Jana Sambarkka Samity against the State of Kerala, the Kerala State Pollution Control Board, and others. The court stated that since there is an alternative and efficacious remedy of appeal under Section 31 of the Air (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1981 and Section 28 of the Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1974, it declines to entertain the writ petition. The petitioners were given liberty to approach the appellate authority within one month from the date of the judgment.

The writ petition was filed by Jana Sambarkka Samity, a non-governmental organization, seeking the court's intervention to stop the alleged illegal dumping of waste in the Kadambrayar river in Kerala. The petitioners claimed that the dumping of waste was causing severe pollution and health hazards to the people living in the surrounding areas.

The court, while dismissing the writ petition, observed that the petitioners have an alternative remedy of appeal under the relevant pollution control laws. The court stated that the appellate authority under the Air and Water Acts has the power to hear and decide on the issues raised by the petitioners. The court further noted that the appellate authority is equipped with the necessary expertise and knowledge to deal with such matters.

The court also emphasized the importance of the principle of exhaustion of remedies, which requires a petitioner to exhaust all available remedies before approaching the court. The court stated that the principle of exhaustion of remedies is a fundamental principle of law and is essential for the proper functioning of the legal system.

The court's decision to dismiss the writ petition and direct the petitioners to approach the appellate authority is in line with the principles of administrative law. The court's decision ensures that the appellate authority, which is the designated authority under the relevant pollution control laws, has the opportunity to hear and decide on the issues raised by the petitioners.

The court's decision also highlights the importance of the role of the appellate authority in the effective implementation of pollution control laws. The appellate authority is equipped with the necessary expertise and knowledge to deal with complex environmental issues and is better placed to ensure that the relevant pollution control laws are implemented effectively.
