In the case of State (WLI) vs Friends Oriental Arts, the accused were charged with violating the Wild Life (Protection) Act, 1972. The court order states that the accused were found in possession of excess ivory articles without any legal source of procurement. The court convicted accused no.1 and accused no.2 and discharged accused no.3 and accused no.4. The court relied on the relevant provisions of the Act and the evidence presented by the complainant and the raiding team. The accused pleaded not guilty and failed to lead any defense evidence.

The court order prohibits a person from acquiring any captive or wild animal, animal article, trophy, uncured trophy, or meat derived from them unless purchased from a dealer or authorized person. The defendant was accused of possessing excess ivory articles without proper documentation. The defense argued that the prosecution failed to prove their case, but the court found the defendant guilty based on the testimony of witnesses and the defendant's admission to being present during the raid. The court also noted that the defendant was required to maintain a register of imported items and failed to provide valid documents for the excess ivory articles seized. The court clarified that there is no distinction between Indian and foreign ivory and that the trade in ivory articles must be based on import.

The court order states that the accused was found in possession of excess ivory articles without any permit or license and was dealing in said articles without maintaining proper register, which is a violation of the Wild Life (Protection) Act. The accused failed to produce valid documents to prove that the excess ivory articles were imported. The defense of the accused that the weighing scale was not accurate and that the case property was not sealed was not accepted by the court. The prosecution has proved its case beyond reasonable doubt, and the accused is convicted for the offense under sections 44, 47, and 48 of the Wild Life (Protection) Act.
