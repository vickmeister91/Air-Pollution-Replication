The High Court recently issued a court order in two criminal revision applications filed by three accused persons who were convicted by a trial court for the offence punishable under Section 51 of the Wild Life (Protection) Act, 1972. The accused were sentenced to three years of rigorous imprisonment and a fine of Rs. 10,000 each. The judgment was challenged before the Sessions Judge, who dismissed the appeals and confirmed the trial court's judgment. The accused then filed the present revision.

The High Court maintained the conviction but modified the sentence to the period already undergone by the accused, considering their young age and the time spent contesting the case. The court order is significant as it highlights the importance of considering the circumstances of the accused while deciding the sentence.

The Wild Life (Protection) Act, 1972, is a crucial legislation that aims to protect wildlife and their habitats. The Act provides for the establishment of national parks, sanctuaries, and other protected areas for the conservation of wildlife. It also prohibits hunting, poaching, and trading of wildlife and their products. Section 51 of the Act deals with the punishment for contravention of the provisions of the Act.

In the present case, the accused were found guilty of hunting a wild boar, which is a protected animal under the Act. The trial court convicted them and sentenced them to three years of rigorous imprisonment and a fine of Rs. 10,000 each. The accused challenged the judgment before the Sessions Judge, who dismissed their appeals and confirmed the trial court's judgment.

The accused then filed a revision before the High Court, challenging their conviction and sentence. The High Court heard the arguments of both parties and upheld the conviction but modified the sentence to the period already undergone by the accused. The court considered the young age of the accused and the time spent contesting the case while deciding the sentence.

The court order is significant as it highlights the importance of considering the circumstances of the accused while deciding the sentence. The court recognized that the accused were young and had spent a considerable amount of time contesting the case. The court also considered the fact that the accused had not committed any other offence and were not habitual offenders.

The court's decision to modify the sentence to the period already undergone by the accused is a welcome move. It shows that the court is willing to consider the circumstances of the accused while deciding the sentence. The court's decision is also in line with the principles of justice and fairness.
