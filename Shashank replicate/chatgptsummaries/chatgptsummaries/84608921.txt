The court order in question pertains to a criminal complaint filed against Gurdeep Singh under various sections of the Punjab Land Preservation Act, 1900 and the Indian Forest Act, 1927. The petitioner had filed a petition under section 482 of the Code of Criminal Procedure seeking to quash the complaint. The court found that the petitioner was not present at the spot when the alleged offence was committed and that the prosecution of the petitioner would be an abuse of the process of court. Therefore, the court allowed the petition and quashed the criminal complaint against the petitioner.

The Punjab Land Preservation Act, 1900 is a law that was enacted to preserve the forests and the environment in the state of Punjab. The act prohibits the cutting of trees, the grazing of cattle, and the cultivation of land in certain areas. The Indian Forest Act, 1927 is a law that was enacted to regulate the use of forests and the trade in forest produce. The act provides for the protection of forests, the prevention of forest fires, and the conservation of wildlife.

In this case, the petitioner, Gurdeep Singh, was accused of violating the provisions of these two acts. The complaint against him alleged that he had cut trees and cultivated land in a forest area without obtaining the necessary permission from the authorities. The petitioner had denied the allegations and had filed a petition under section 482 of the Code of Criminal Procedure seeking to quash the complaint.

Section 482 of the Code of Criminal Procedure empowers the high court to exercise its inherent powers to prevent the abuse of the process of court or to secure the ends of justice. The section provides that nothing in the code shall be deemed to limit or affect the inherent powers of the high court to make such orders as may be necessary to give effect to any order under the code, or to prevent abuse of the process of any court or otherwise to secure the ends of justice.

In this case, the court found that the petitioner was not present at the spot when the alleged offence was committed. The court also found that the prosecution of the petitioner would be an abuse of the process of court. The court observed that the complaint against the petitioner was filed after a delay of several years and that there was no evidence to show that the petitioner had committed the alleged offence. The court also noted that the petitioner had obtained the necessary permission from the authorities for the cultivation of land in the area.

Based on these findings, the court allowed the petition and quashed the criminal complaint against the petitioner. The court held that the complaint was without merit and that the petitioner had been falsely implicated in the case. The court also observed that the prosecution of the petitioner would have caused undue harassment to him and would have been a waste of the court's time and resources.

In conclusion, the court order in this case highlights the importance of the inherent powers of the high court to prevent the abuse of the process of court and to secure the ends of justice.
