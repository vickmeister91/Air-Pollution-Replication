The Gujarat High Court recently considered a petition challenging a lower court's decision to reject an application to stop criminal proceedings against the petitioners for violating the Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1974. The petitioners argued that the court was incompetent to take cognizance of the case because the State Board's written sanction was not produced along with the complaint. However, the court held that non-production of the written sanction along with the complaint did not constitute a legal fetter in the maintainability of the complaint. The court emphasized that technical flaws or procedural infirmities should not hamper or constitute a hurdle in the way of the prosecution, especially in cases involving serious offenses that affect public health.

The court order highlights the importance of not allowing technical flaws in a complaint to be used by industrialists as a weapon to avoid prosecution. The court cites a similar case where a large business house was allowed to escape consequences due to negligence in drafting the complaint. The court notes that technical flaws in a complaint can be allowed to be removed, but they should not be used as an excuse to avoid prosecution.

The court rejected a revision application and directed the trial magistrate to accord top priority to the matter and dispose of it as early as possible. The court also rejected a request to extend the stay order. The court's decision sends a strong message that technical flaws or procedural infirmities should not be used as an excuse to avoid prosecution in cases involving serious offenses that affect public health.

The Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1974, is a crucial piece of legislation that aims to prevent and control water pollution. The act provides for the establishment of pollution control boards at the central and state levels and lays down guidelines for the prevention and control of water pollution. The act also provides for the punishment of those who violate its provisions.

The court's decision is significant because it upholds the importance of the act and ensures that those who violate its provisions are held accountable. The court's emphasis on not allowing technical flaws or procedural infirmities to hamper or constitute a hurdle in the way of the prosecution is a welcome move. It ensures that those who violate the act are held accountable and that public health is protected.
