Canara Distributors, a company accused of committing an offense under Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act, filed a criminal petition to set aside an order in the case. The respondent had filed a private complaint alleging that Canara Distributors had borrowed Rs. 10,00,000/- and failed to repay it. The petitioner had filed two applications, one under Section 91 of Cr.P.C. and another under Section 293 of Cr.P.C. r/w Section 45 of the Indian Evidence Act, which were dismissed by the trial court. The High Court rejected the criminal petition, stating that there was no good ground to entertain it.

The case involved a private complaint filed by the respondent against Canara Distributors, alleging that the company had borrowed Rs. 10,00,000/- and failed to repay it. The petitioner had filed two applications, one under Section 91 of Cr.P.C. and another under Section 293 of Cr.P.C. r/w Section 45 of the Indian Evidence Act, which were dismissed by the trial court. The petitioner then filed a criminal petition to set aside the order.

The High Court rejected the criminal petition, stating that there was no good ground to entertain it. The court noted that the petitioner had failed to produce any material to show that the order passed by the trial court was illegal or improper. The court also observed that the petitioner had not made out any case for interference in the order passed by the trial court.

The court further noted that the petitioner had failed to produce any material to show that the respondent had filed the private complaint with a mala fide intention. The court observed that the respondent had filed the complaint after the petitioner had failed to repay the amount borrowed. The court also noted that the petitioner had not produced any material to show that the respondent had filed the complaint with a view to harass or pressurize the petitioner.

The court also observed that the petitioner had not made out any case for quashing the complaint filed by the respondent. The court noted that the complaint disclosed the commission of an offense under Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act and that the trial court had rightly taken cognizance of the same.

The court further observed that the petitioner had not made out any case for invoking the provisions of Section 91 of Cr.P.C. The court noted that the petitioner had failed to show that the documents sought to be produced were relevant and necessary for the purpose of the case.

The court also observed that the petitioner had not made out any case for invoking the provisions of Section 293 of Cr.P.C. r/w Section 45 of the Indian Evidence Act. The court noted that the petitioner had failed to show that the documents sought to be produced were public documents and that their production was necessary for the purpose of the case.

In conclusion, the High Court rejected the criminal petition filed by Canara Distributors, stating that there was no good ground to entertain it. The court observed that the petitioner had failed to produce any material to show that the order passed by the trial court was illegal or improper. The court also observed that the petitioner had not made out any case for interference in the order passed by the trial court. The court further observed that the petitioner had not made out any case for quashing the complaint filed by the respondent or for invoking the provisions of Section 91 of Cr.P.C. or Section 293 of Cr.P.C. r/w Section 45 of the Indian Evidence Act.
