The court order in the case of Yesu Bhavan v. 6th respondent pertains to the challenge against the consent to operate a hot mix plant granted to the 6th respondent. The petitioner, Yesu Bhavan, had filed a writ petition challenging the consent order, but the court found that the petitioner had an effective alternate remedy by way of an appeal before the Appellate Authority under the Air (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act.

The court observed that the Air (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act provides for a comprehensive mechanism for the prevention and control of air pollution. The Act establishes various authorities, including the State Pollution Control Board, the Appellate Authority, and the National Green Tribunal, to ensure compliance with the provisions of the Act.

The court noted that the petitioner had not exhausted the remedy of filing an appeal before the Appellate Authority before approaching the court. The court held that the petitioner must first avail of the remedy provided under the Act before seeking relief from the court.

The court further observed that the Appellate Authority is empowered to consider all aspects of the matter, including the environmental impact of the hot mix plant, before passing orders. The court held that the Appellate Authority is the appropriate forum to adjudicate on the matter and that the petitioner must approach the Appellate Authority within ten days to file an appeal.

The court directed the Appellate Authority to consider the appeal and pass orders within one month of the filing of the appeal. The court also dismissed the writ petition challenging the consent order, as the petitioner had an effective alternate remedy available under the Act.

The court's order highlights the importance of exhausting all available remedies before approaching the court. The court observed that the Appellate Authority is the appropriate forum to adjudicate on matters related to the prevention and control of air pollution, and that the petitioner must first approach the Appellate Authority before seeking relief from the court.

The court's order also underscores the need for effective implementation of environmental laws and regulations.
